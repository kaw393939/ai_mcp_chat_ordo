# Chapter 0 — The Thread: From Accommodation to Universal Design

## Abstract

Accessibility is not charity. It is not compliance. It is the discipline of building products that work for everyone — including the 1.3 billion people worldwide who experience significant disability. This book traces accessibility from the civil rights framework through assistive technology innovation, WCAG standards, inclusive design methodology, and the engineering practices that make accessibility a quality attribute rather than a retrofit.

---

> **A note about this book:**
> Book III (UI Design, Chapter 8) introduced accessibility as a component engineering concern. This book expands that scope to cover the full discipline: legal requirements, design methodology, testing practices, organizational change, and the philosophical framework that distinguishes *accommodating disability* from *designing for human diversity*.

## The Arc of Accessibility

- **1970s**: Section 504 — the first US law prohibiting disability discrimination in federally funded programs
- **1990**: Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) — extending disability rights to the private sector
- **1999**: WCAG 1.0 — the first web accessibility guidelines
- **2006**: UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities
- **2008**: WCAG 2.0 — the technology-neutral, testable guidelines
- **2018**: WCAG 2.1 — adding mobile, low vision, and cognitive disability requirements
- **2023**: European Accessibility Act enforcement deadline — making accessibility legally required across the EU

## Reader Exercise: The Five-Minute Audit

Turn off your mouse. Navigate your product using only Tab, Shift+Tab, Enter, Space, Escape, and arrow keys. Can you reach every interactive element? Can you activate every button? Can you close every modal? This five-minute exercise reveals more accessibility issues than most automated tools.
