# Chapter 1 — The Rights Framework: Why Accessibility Is Not Optional

## Abstract

Accessibility is a civil right, not a feature request. This chapter traces the legal and ethical framework from Section 504 through the ADA, the UN Convention, and modern enforcement actions that make accessibility a legal requirement in most markets.

---

## Ed Roberts and the Independent Living Movement (1960s)

**Ed Roberts** contracted polio at age 14 and used a wheelchair and an iron lung for the rest of his life. In 1962, he became the first wheelchair user to attend UC Berkeley. The university initially refused to admit him because they had no accessible housing. He moved into the campus hospital.

Roberts's fight for access at Berkeley launched the **independent living movement** — the principle that people with disabilities have the right to live independently, make their own decisions, and participate fully in society. This movement directly produced Section 504 (1973), which prohibited disability discrimination in federally funded programs, and ultimately the ADA (1990).

Roberts established the framework that governs digital accessibility today: access is a *right*, not a *favor*. Products that exclude people with disabilities are not incomplete — they are discriminatory.

**What frustrated him:** The assumption that disability was a medical problem to be fixed rather than a social problem to be addressed through design.

## The Legal Landscape

| Law/Standard | Year | Scope | Key Requirement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Section 504 | 1973 | US federal programs | No discrimination based on disability |
| ADA | 1990 | US public/private | Accessible public accommodations |
| Section 508 | 1998 (updated 2017) | US federal IT | WCAG 2.0 AA compliance for federal technology |
| WCAG 2.0 | 2008 | International | Technology-neutral accessibility guidelines |
| WCAG 2.1 | 2018 | International | Added mobile, low vision, cognitive requirements |
| European Accessibility Act | 2019 (enforced 2025) | EU | Accessibility required for products and services |

The direction is clear: legal requirements for digital accessibility are expanding, not contracting. Products that ignore accessibility today will face mandatory remediation tomorrow.

## The Social Model of Disability

The **social model** distinguishes between *impairment* (a physical or cognitive difference) and *disability* (the barriers that society creates). A person who uses a wheelchair is not disabled by their legs — they are disabled by stairs. A person who is blind is not disabled by their eyes — they are disabled by interfaces that require sight.

This reframing transforms accessibility from "helping disabled people" to "removing barriers from the environment." The barrier is in the product, not in the person.
