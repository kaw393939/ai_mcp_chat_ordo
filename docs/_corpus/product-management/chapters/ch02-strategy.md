# Chapter 2 — Strategy: Positioning and Competitive Advantage

## Abstract

Product strategy is the discipline of choosing *where to play* and *how to win*. This chapter covers Michael Porter's competitive forces, April Dunford's positioning framework, and the blue ocean strategy — all applied to software products in the AI era.

---

## Michael Porter and Competitive Strategy (1979)

**Michael Porter** defined five forces that determine industry profitability: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitutes, and competitive rivalry. Every product exists within these forces.

For software products, Porter's forces map to: barriers to entry (technical moats vs. commodity code), platform dependency (supplier power of Apple, Google, AWS), user switching costs (buyer power), alternative solutions (substitutes), and direct competitors (rivalry).

## April Dunford and Obviously Awesome (2019)

**April Dunford** published *Obviously Awesome*, arguing that positioning is the most undermanaged element in product strategy. Her framework: define your competitive alternatives (what would customers use if you didn't exist?), then articulate your unique attributes, the value those attributes enable, and the target customer who cares most about that value.

**What frustrated her:** Products that positioned themselves by their features rather than by the problem they solved uniquely well.
