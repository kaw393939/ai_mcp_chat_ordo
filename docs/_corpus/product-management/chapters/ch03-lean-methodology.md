# Chapter 3 — Lean Methodology: Hypothesis-Driven Development

## Abstract

The lean startup methodology transformed product development from "plan, build, hope" to "hypothesize, test, learn." This chapter covers Eric Ries's *The Lean Startup*, the Build-Measure-Learn loop, minimum viable products, and the pivot-or-persevere decision.

---

## Eric Ries and the Lean Startup (2011)

**Eric Ries** published *The Lean Startup* in 2011, synthesizing lean manufacturing, agile development, and customer development into a framework for reducing startup waste.

His core insight: the biggest risk in product development is not technical failure — it is building something nobody wants. Traditional planning (write a business plan, build the product, launch) assumes you know what customers want. Lean methodology assumes you *don't* — and provides a scientific method for finding out.

### The Build-Measure-Learn Loop

1. **Build**: create a minimum viable experiment to test a hypothesis
2. **Measure**: collect data on the hypothesis
3. **Learn**: determine whether the hypothesis was validated or invalidated

The critical insight: the loop runs *backwards*. You start by defining what you need to **learn**, then determine what to **measure** to learn it, then determine what to **build** to generate that measurement.

### The MVP

A minimum viable product is not a low-quality product. It is the *smallest experiment that can test a critical hypothesis*. A landing page that measures sign-up intent. A concierge service that manually delivers what the product would automate. A Wizard of Oz prototype where a human performs what the algorithm would do.

**What frustrated him:** Teams that built complete products before validating any assumption about customer behavior.

## The Pivot-or-Persevere Decision

After enough learning cycles, the team faces a binary choice: **pivot** (change strategy while maintaining vision) or **persevere** (continue the current strategy with refinements).

Types of pivots: zoom-in (one feature becomes the product), zoom-out (the product becomes a feature of something larger), customer segment (same product, different audience), channel (different distribution method), value capture (different revenue model).

## Chapter Checklist

- Can you articulate the hypothesis behind your current sprint's work?
- Is your MVP testing a specific assumption, or is it a small version of the full product?
- Have you defined "validated" and "invalidated" criteria before running experiments?
- When was the last time your team made a pivot-or-persevere decision?
