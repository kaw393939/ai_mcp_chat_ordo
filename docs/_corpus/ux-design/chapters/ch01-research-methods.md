# Chapter 1 — Research Methods: Knowing Before Building

## Abstract

The most expensive mistake in product development is building the right thing wrong. The *most expensive* mistake is building the wrong thing well. UX research exists to prevent the second mistake. This chapter traces research methodology from ethnographic fieldwork through Jakob Nielsen's discount usability, Erika Hall's *Just Enough Research*, and modern remote, unmoderated, and AI-assisted research methods.

---

## Jakob Nielsen and Discount Usability (1989)

**Jakob Nielsen** proved that usability testing does not require a laboratory. His most famous finding: **five users are enough to find 85% of usability problems.** This single insight democratized usability testing — teams no longer needed expensive labs and large participant pools.

Nielsen's "discount usability" methods:

- **Heuristic evaluation**: experts evaluate the interface against ten usability heuristics
- **Thinking aloud**: users narrate their thought process while completing tasks
- **Simplified iterations**: test, fix, test again — with five users each round

His ten usability heuristics remain the standard evaluation framework:

1. Visibility of system status
2. Match between system and real world
3. User control and freedom
4. Consistency and standards
5. Error prevention
6. Recognition rather than recall
7. Flexibility and efficiency of use
8. Aesthetic and minimalist design
9. Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors
10. Help and documentation

**What frustrated him:** Teams that shipped products without watching a single user try to use them.

## Erika Hall and Just Enough Research (2013)

**Erika Hall** (Mule Design) published *Just Enough Research* to counter two failure modes: teams that do *no* research and teams that do *endless* research.

Her key principles:

- **Research is a design activity**: it produces decisions, not reports
- **Time-box everything**: any research is better than no research, and two weeks of research is almost always sufficient
- **Ask "why," not "what"**: surveys tell you what users do; interviews tell you why

Hall's framework for choosing research methods:

| Question | Method |
| ---------- | -------- |
| What do users *do*? | Analytics, session recordings, heatmaps |
| What do users *say* they do? | Surveys, interviews |
| What do users *need*? | Contextual inquiry, diary studies |
| What do users *struggle with*? | Usability testing, task analysis |
| What do users *feel*? | Interviews, satisfaction surveys (CSAT, NPS) |

**What frustrated her:** Research used as procrastination — teams that stayed in research mode to avoid the risk of actually building something.

## Research Methods in Practice

### Qualitative Methods (Understanding Why)

- **Contextual inquiry**: observing users in their natural environment
- **Semi-structured interviews**: guided conversation with flexibility for unexpected insights
- **Card sorting**: users organize content into categories that make sense to them
- **Diary studies**: users document their experiences over time

### Quantitative Methods (Measuring What)

- **Analytics**: page views, conversion funnels, drop-off rates
- **A/B testing**: comparing two variations with statistically significant sample sizes
- **Task success rate**: percentage of users who complete a defined task
- **System Usability Scale (SUS)**: standardized 10-question usability questionnaire

### The Mixed-Methods Principle

Neither qualitative nor quantitative alone is sufficient. Qualitative tells you *what's happening and why*. Quantitative tells you *how often and how severely*. Every research program needs both.

---

## What This Means for Us

Research is the mechanism that converts assumptions into evidence. Without it, design decisions are preferences pretending to be principles.

## Chapter Checklist

- Have you watched at least five users attempt core tasks before shipping?
- Are research findings producing *decisions*, or are they producing reports that sit unread?
- Do you have both qualitative data (why) and quantitative data (how much)?
- Is research time-boxed to prevent analysis paralysis?
- Can you articulate the top three user frustrations — with evidence?
