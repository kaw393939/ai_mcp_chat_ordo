# Chapter 2 — Inclusive Design: Designing for Human Diversity

## Abstract
Inclusive design is not the same as accessibility compliance. Accessibility asks "does this meet WCAG?" Inclusive design asks "who are we excluding, and why?" This chapter covers Kat Holmes's *Mismatch*, the Microsoft Inclusive Design toolkit, and the spectrum of permanent, temporary, and situational disability.

---

## Kat Holmes and Mismatch (2018)

**Kat Holmes** led Microsoft's Inclusive Design practice and published *Mismatch* in 2018. Her central argument: exclusion is not always intentional. It is often the result of *mismatched* interactions — the product was designed for one type of body, one type of cognition, or one type of context, and everyone outside that design assumption is excluded.

### The Disability Spectrum
Holmes introduced the concept that disability exists on a spectrum of permanence:

| Permanent | Temporary | Situational |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| One arm | Arm in sling | Holding a baby |
| Blind | Eye infection | Driving a car |
| Deaf | Ear infection | Loud concert |
| Non-verbal | Laryngitis | Foreign language context |

Designing for the permanent case benefits all three. Subtitles designed for deaf users help people in loud airports. Voice control designed for motor-impaired users helps people with full hands. This is the **curb-cut effect**: curb cuts were mandated for wheelchair users, but they benefit everyone (strollers, luggage, bikes).

**What frustrated her:** Teams that treated accessibility as a checklist for compliance rather than a design philosophy for inclusion.

## Chapter Checklist
- Can you identify who your product currently excludes — and why?
- Do you design for permanent, temporary, and situational disability?
- Is inclusive design part of your design process from the start, or retrofitted after?
