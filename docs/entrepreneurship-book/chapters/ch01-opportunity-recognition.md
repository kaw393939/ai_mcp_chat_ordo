# Chapter 1 — Opportunity Recognition: Seeing What Others Miss

## Abstract
Every successful company started with someone noticing a problem that others overlooked or accepted as inevitable. This chapter covers Paul Graham's essay framework, Peter Thiel's contrarian question, and the discipline of distinguishing real problems from imagined ones.

---

## Paul Graham and the Essay-Driven Startup (2005)

**Paul Graham** co-founded Y Combinator and wrote the most influential essay corpus in startup culture. His key insight: the best startup ideas are always **obvious in retrospect** — but they are only visible to people who have *direct experience with the problem*.

Graham's formula: "Make something people want." Not something you *think* people want. Not something investors want. Something *people* — real users with real frustrations — demonstrably want, evidenced by their behavior, not their words.

**What frustrated him:** Founders who started with a technology and went looking for a problem to solve, rather than starting with a problem and finding the simplest technology to solve it.

## Peter Thiel and the Contrarian Question

**Peter Thiel** (PayPal, Palantir, Founders Fund) poses one question to every potential investment: "What important truth do few people agree with you on?" Applied to entrepreneurship, this becomes: what problem exists that most people don't see or believe can be solved?

## Chapter Checklist
- Can you describe the problem you're solving from the *user's* perspective?
- Is your insight based on direct experience, or on a theory?
- Have at least 10 potential users told you (unprompted) that this problem is painful?
